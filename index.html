The Crisis

A country still rebuilding — and a generation left behind

Kosovo declared independence in 2008, following the devastating 1999 war. It remains one of Europe's youngest and poorest nations — with healthcare infrastructure, social services, and disability support still far from where families need them.

For parents of children with intellectual and developmental disabilities (IDD), the reality is stark: they must work to survive, but there is no one to care for their child while they do. Every day, they leave home with fear.

"They cannot call for help. They cannot recognize danger. They cannot tell a stranger their name or where they live."

The daily reality for IDD families in Kosovo

They cannot call for help

Children aged 9–15 with IDD often cannot use a phone, communicate clearly with strangers, or understand that they are in danger. When something goes wrong, they are completely alone.

Parents have no way of knowing

A parent at work 40 minutes away has no system to alert them if their child falls, wanders outside, or opens the door to a stranger. They find out when they come home.

The absences can last all day

Some parents leave for 30 minutes. Others for a full 8-hour workday. In either case, the child is entirely alone with no safety net of any kind.

No services exist to fill the gap

Unlike in Western Europe or the US, Kosovo has almost no state-funded disability respite, home care, or community support services for these families. They are on their own.

Our Solution

Safety technology, built for the real world

We are developing two complementary technologies — starting with the simplest, most proven approach, and building toward a full safety system. Every phase is designed to work on Kosovo's infrastructure, at a cost families can sustain.

Phase 1 — Active now

GPS Location Tracker

A small, durable wristband worn by the child. It connects via mobile data — no home WiFi needed. The parent texts one word and instantly receives the child's location as a map link. An SOS button lets the child call for help with one press. If the child leaves a safe zone, the parent is alerted immediately.

Building now · 2–4 months to pilot

Phase 2 — Coming next

In-Home Sensor System

A set of simple sensors installed in the home — a door sensor, smoke detector, and motion sensor — that send SMS alerts to the parent if something is wrong. No AI, no complexity. Just immediate alerts for the dangers that matter most: fire, wandering, and prolonged inactivity.

Planned · 6–12 months

Phase 3 — Future

AI Danger Detection

An AI camera that detects falls, stove hazards, and unknown visitors — processing everything locally on the device for privacy. This is the most powerful and most complex option. We will build it once the simpler systems are trusted by families.

Year 2+

Our principle

Simple first. Trust before complexity.

Every phase starts with the most proven, lowest-cost version of the technology. Families need to trust the system before we add features. A GPS tracker that families actually use every day is worth more than an AI camera that sits unplugged.

OM Kosovo · Field Representative

On the ground since 1999

OM Kosovo is the local branch of Operation Mobilisation — an international humanitarian organization operating in over 110 countries. They entered Kosovo immediately after the 1999 war to provide aid to returning refugees, and have maintained a continuous presence ever since.

Their focus is Kosovo's most vulnerable: marginalized women, children, and youth at risk. They have deep, trusted relationships with local families — which is exactly what our project needs to reach IDD communities and deploy technology responsibly.

OM Kosovo serves as our official partner and fiscal sponsor — meaning all donations are received and managed through their registered NGO, ensuring full legal accountability and transparency.

About Levah

A student-led initiative with a real plan

Levah was founded by a high school student in Virginia with a simple conviction: that children with disabilities in Kosovo deserve the same safety their peers in wealthier countries take for granted. The name comes from two Hebrew roots — lev, meaning heart, and lavah, meaning to join — because this project is about connecting people who care with families who need it most.

The initiative operates under school supervision and in partnership with OM Kosovo. We are currently in the research and pilot planning phase, with the first GPS tracker deployment targeted for late 2026.
